Sandalwood Defoliator Moth

Amata passalis

🚨 Pest

Common Names: Sandalwood Defoliator, Handmaiden Moth, Indian Wasp Moth

Local Name: சந்தன இலையுதிர் அந்துப்பூச்சி (Sandana Ilaiyudir Andhuppūchchi)

Family: Erebidae

Order: Lepidoptera

Size: 30–40 mm wingspan

Activity: Diurnal (day-flying)

🔍 Identification
AppearanceSlender, wasp-like moth with narrow dark wings marked by translucent white or yellowish spots. Abdomen banded with bright orange-yellow and black rings.
ColourBlack wings with white or pale yellow translucent patches; abdomen black with orange-yellow bands
Distinguishing FeaturesWasp-mimicking appearance with narrow wings, bright abdominal banding, and slow buzzing flight during daytime
Similar SpeciesAmata cyssea (Handmaiden Moth) — distinguished by wing spot pattern and abdominal band count
🌿 Habitat & Behaviour
HabitatOrchards, gardens, sandalwood plantations, pulse crop fields, and ornamental gardens across India and Sri Lanka
BehaviourDay-flying moth with slow, buzzing flight; mimics wasps to deter predators; larvae are gregarious feeders
Activity PatternActive during daylight hours; adults emerge within 1–2 hours of sunrise
DietLarvae feed on sandalwood leaves, cowpeas, pulses, and ornamental plants; adults feed on nectar
Seasonal PresenceYear-round breeding in tropical climates; 6–11 generations per year
Host PlantsSandalwood (Santalum album), cowpea (Vigna unguiculata), French bean (Phaseolus vulgaris), snake gourd, capsicum
🔄 Lifecycle
Egg StageFemale lays about 305 eggs in clusters on host plant leaves over a lifespan of ~4 days
Larval StageEight larval instars; first instar ~2 mm, final instar ~29 mm; hairy caterpillars that feed gregariously
Pupal StagePupation occurs in a loose cocoon among leaf litter or soil
Adult StageAdults emerge at sunrise; mating occurs within a day of emergence; average life cycle ~62 days
Generations per Year6–11 generations per year; breeds year-round in tropical regions
🌾 Orchard Impact
Impact TypePest — larvae cause severe defoliation
Crops AffectedSandalwood, cowpeas, French beans, snake gourd, capsicum, and ornamental plants
Damage DescriptionGregarious larvae skeletonize and defoliate leaves, reducing photosynthesis and weakening the plant; severe infestations can strip entire branches
Damage SeverityHigh in sandalwood plantations; moderate in pulse crops
🛡️ Pest Status & Safety
Pest ClassificationMajor pest of sandalwood; minor pest of pulses and vegetables
Danger to HumansNone — harmless to humans; larvae may cause mild skin irritation due to body hairs
Invasive StatusNative to India and Sri Lanka; not considered invasive
🚫 Prevention & Control
Physical ControlHand-pick and destroy egg clusters and gregarious larvae from infested leaves
Biological ControlParasitoid wasp Apanteles nepitae is effective against larvae; encourage natural predators
Organic SpraysNeem oil spray on young larvae; Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) spray for caterpillar control
Early Warning SignsSkeletonized leaves, presence of hairy caterpillars feeding in groups, day-flying wasp-like moths near host plants
