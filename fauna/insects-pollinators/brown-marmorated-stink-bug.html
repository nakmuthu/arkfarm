Brown Marmorated Stink Bug

Halyomorpha halys

🚨 Pest ⚠️ Medium Risk to Crops 🌍 Invasive

Common Names: Brown Marmorated Stink Bug, BMSB

Local Name: பழுப்பு நிற வாசனை வண்டு

Family: Pentatomidae

Order: Hemiptera

Size: 12–16 mm

Activity: Diurnal; gregarious

🔍 Identification
AppearanceShield-shaped body with mottled brown coloration; marbled pattern on wing covers; banded antennae and legs
ColourMottled brown with lighter and darker patches; underside lighter
Distinguishing FeaturesAlternating light/dark banding on antennae; smooth shoulder edges; emits strong unpleasant odour when disturbed
EggsBarrel-shaped, pale green; laid in clusters of 20–30 on leaf undersides
🌿 Habitat & Behaviour
HabitatOrchards, gardens, agricultural fields, forests; highly adaptable; prefers fruit trees and vegetable crops
BehaviourGregarious; aggregates on host plants; strong fliers; migrate seasonally; overwinter in protected structures
DietPhytophagous — pierces fruit, leaves, and stems with stylet to feed on plant sap; wide host range including apples, pears, peaches, citrus, berries
Seasonal PresenceSpring to autumn; overwinters as adults; 2–3 generations per year
🔄 Lifecycle
Egg StageClusters of 20–30 barrel-shaped eggs on leaf undersides; hatch in 4–5 days
Nymph Stage5 instars over 4–6 weeks; nymphs are brightly coloured and become more brown with age
Adult StageAdults overwinter; live several months; 2–3 generations per year in warm climates
OverwinteringAdults seek sheltered locations (under bark, in buildings) to overwinter
🌾 Orchard Impact
Impact TypePest — significant crop damage
Crops AffectedApples, pears, peaches, citrus, berries, figs, grapes, vegetables
Damage DescriptionPiercing feeding causes dimpling, discolouration, and corky tissue under fruit skin; makes fruit unmarketable
Damage SeverityHigh — can cause 30–90% crop loss in severe infestations
🛡️ Pest Status & Safety
Pest ClassificationMajor agricultural pest; invasive species in many regions
Danger to HumansLow — not venomous; unpleasant odour when crushed; may cause mild skin irritation
Invasive StatusInvasive — native to East Asia; introduced to North America, Europe, and other regions
🚫 Prevention & Control
Physical ControlExclusion netting over fruit trees; hand-picking and dropping in soapy water; sticky traps
Pheromone TrapsAggregation pheromone traps for monitoring and mass trapping
Biological ControlParasitoid wasps (Trissolcus japonicus); encourage natural predators (birds, spiders)
Organic SpraysNeem oil; insecticidal soap; kaolin clay on fruit surfaces
Early Warning SignsDimpled or discoloured fruit; unpleasant odour; aggregations on sunny sides of trees in autumn
