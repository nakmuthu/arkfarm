Lime Butterfly

Papilio demoleus

🚨 Pest (Larva) ✅ Beneficial (Adult) ⚠️ Medium Risk to Citrus

Common Names: Lime Butterfly, Lemon Butterfly, Chequered Swallowtail, Citrus Swallowtail

Tamil Name: எலுமிச்சை பூச்சி

Family: Papilionidae

Order: Lepidoptera

Category: Insects & Pollinators

Wingspan: 80–100 mm

Activity: Diurnal

🔍 Identification
Adult AppearanceLarge butterfly with black wings patterned with white/cream patches and spots; red eyespots on hindwings; no tail projections (unlike most swallowtails)
Wingspan80–100 mm
Larva AppearanceEarly instars resemble bird droppings (black/white); later instars bright green with white diagonal bands and orange eyespots on thorax
PupaGreen or brown chrysalis attached to twigs; camouflaged
EggsPale yellow, spherical; laid singly on young leaves of host plants
🌿 Habitat & Behaviour
HabitatOrchards (especially citrus), gardens, forests, cultivated areas; widespread across South and Southeast Asia
Adult BehaviourStrong fliers; males patrol territories; attracted to flowers for nectar; active during day
Larval BehaviourFeeds voraciously on citrus leaves; uses bird-dropping mimicry for protection; later instars use osmeterium (orange forked organ) to deter predators
Host PlantsCitrus species (lemon, lime, orange), Curry Leaf (Murraya koenigii), Bael (Aegle marmelos), Toddalia
Seasonal PresenceYear-round in tropical climates; 3–4 generations per year
🔄 Lifecycle
Egg StageLaid singly on young leaves; hatch in 3–5 days
Larval Stage4–5 instars over 2–3 weeks; feeds on citrus leaves
Pupal StageChrysalis stage 1–2 weeks; attached to twigs or stems
Adult Stage2–3 weeks; feeds on nectar; mates and lays eggs
Generations per Year3–4 generations in tropical climates
🌾 Orchard Impact
Impact TypeDual — pest (larva) and beneficial (adult)
Crops AffectedCitrus (lemon, lime, orange, sweet lime), Curry Leaf
Larval DamageDefoliation of young citrus shoots; can strip new growth completely; multiple generations cause cumulative damage on young trees
Adult BenefitImportant pollinator; visits flowers of many orchard species
Damage SeverityModerate on young trees; established trees tolerate some defoliation
🛡️ Pest Status & Safety
Pest ClassificationMinor to moderate pest on citrus; important pollinator as adult
Danger to HumansNone — completely harmless; larvae may emit unpleasant smell from osmeterium when threatened
Danger to LivestockNone
Invasive StatusNative to South and Southeast Asia; introduced to some other regions
🚫 Prevention & Control
Physical ControlHand-pick eggs and young larvae from citrus leaves; check undersides of young leaves regularly
Biological ControlEncourage parasitoid wasps (Trichogramma spp.); birds naturally predate larvae; spiders catch adults
Organic SpraysBacillus thuringiensis (Bt) spray on young larvae; neem oil on egg clusters
Cultural PracticesMonitor young citrus flushes; protect young trees with netting; maintain diverse orchard to support natural predators
ThresholdControl only when larval numbers are high on young trees; established trees can tolerate moderate defoliation
Early Warning SignsEggs on young citrus leaves; bird-dropping-like early instar larvae; chewed young shoots
