Brown Stink Bug

Euschistus servus

🚨 Pest

Common Names: Brown Stink Bug

Local Name: பழுப்பு நாற்றப் பூச்சி (Pazhuppu Nāṟṟap Pūchchi)

Family: Pentatomidae

Order: Hemiptera

Size: 10–15 mm

Activity: Diurnal

🔍 Identification
AppearanceBroad, flattened, shield-shaped bug with piercing-sucking mouthparts. Grayish-yellow to brown body with dark punctures on the dorsal surface.
ColourGrayish-brown to yellowish-brown with dark punctures on back; pinkish tinge on ventral surface; darker 4th and 5th antennal segments
Distinguishing FeaturesRounded humeral angles on pronotum; large cheeks passing the clypeus; yellowish antennal bands; releases foul odour when disturbed
Similar SpeciesBrown Marmorated Stink Bug (Halyomorpha halys) — distinguished by rounded vs. pointed shoulders and less apparent antennal bands
🌿 Habitat & Behaviour
HabitatOrchards, crop fields, pecan groves, soybean and cotton fields; overwinters in ditch banks, fence rows, and leaf litter
BehaviourFeeds on developing fruits and seeds using piercing-sucking mouthparts; releases defensive stink when disturbed; overwinters as adult
Activity PatternActive during warm months; emerges from overwintering sites in spring during bloom and shuck split
DietPolyphagous — peaches, pecans, soybeans, cotton, corn, tomatoes, green beans, and various fruit and grain crops
Seasonal PresenceSpring through autumn; may remain active year-round in mild winters in southern regions
Host PlantsPeach, Pecan, Soybean, Cotton, Corn, Tomato, Green Bean, and various fruit and nut trees
🔄 Lifecycle
Egg StageEggs laid in clusters on leaf undersides; yellowish-translucent, turning light pink before hatching
Nymph Stage5 instars over approximately 29 days; nymphs are pale green, oval, and smaller than adults
Adult StageAdults overwinter in protected areas; emerge in spring to feed and reproduce
Generations per YearMultivoltine — 2 or more generations per year depending on climate
OverwinteringAdults overwinter under leaf litter, in ditch banks, fence rows, and other protected sites
🌾 Orchard Impact
Impact TypePest — causes catfacing on fruit and kernel damage in nuts
Crops AffectedPeaches, pecans, soybeans, cotton, corn, tomatoes, green beans, and other seed, grain, nut, and fruit crops
Damage DescriptionPierces developing fruit causing catfacing scars on peaches; feeds on pecan kernels causing dark spots; damages corn seedlings and soybean pods
Damage SeverityHigh — serious pest in southern U.S.; sporadic but can cause significant damage especially under drought conditions
🛡️ Pest Status & Safety
Pest ClassificationSerious polyphagous pest of fruit, grain, nut, and seed crops
Danger to HumansNone — harmless to humans; releases a foul-smelling defensive secretion when disturbed
Invasive StatusNative to North America; widespread across southern Canada and the United States
🚫 Prevention & Control
Physical ControlHand-pick adults from plants; remove overwintering habitat such as dense weeds and leaf litter near orchards
Pheromone TrapsAggregation pheromone traps can be used for monitoring population levels and timing control measures
Biological ControlEgg parasitoids (Telenomus and Trissolcus species) provide natural control; encourage parasitoid habitat
Organic SpraysKaolin clay spray as a deterrent; pyrethrin-based sprays for heavy infestations
ThresholdTreat when populations exceed economic thresholds; scout during bloom and fruit development stages
Early Warning SignsCatfacing scars on developing fruit; dark kernel spots on pecans; stink bugs on trap crops or field edges
