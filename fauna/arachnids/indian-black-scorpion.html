Indian Black Scorpion

Deccanometrus bengalensis

⚠️ Medium Risk 🔵 Neutral

Common Names: Indian Black Scorpion, Bengal Black Scorpion

Local Name: கருந்தேள் (Karundhēḷ)

Family: Scorpionidae

Order: Scorpiones

Size: 95–115 mm

Activity: Nocturnal

🔍 Identification
AppearanceLarge, robust scorpion with broad pedipalps (pincers) and a thick metasoma (tail). Hairy, spherical telson with a poison bladder longer than the stinger.
ColourDark reddish-brown to light brown body; chelae (pincers) with uneven surfaces
Distinguishing FeaturesLarge size (up to 115 mm); pedipalps narrower and longer in males; chelae without pronounced granules or keels; pecten with 14–17 teeth
Similar SpeciesOther Deccanometrus species (D. phipsoni, D. xanthopus); distinguished by chelae surface texture and geographic range
🌿 Habitat & Behaviour
HabitatDry scrubland, edges of ditches, and areas with thick vegetation and shrubs providing shade and cover; native to West Bengal and Odisha
BehaviourNocturnal ambush predator; hides under rocks, logs, and debris during the day; solitary and territorial; uses pincers to capture prey
Activity PatternStrictly nocturnal; emerges after dark to hunt; retreats to burrows or cover at dawn
DietCarnivorous — insects, spiders, small invertebrates; occasionally small lizards and other scorpions
Seasonal PresenceYear-round; more active during warm, humid months; may shelter deeper during extreme heat or cold
🔄 Lifecycle
Egg StageViviparous — females give birth to live young (no egg stage); gestation period several months
Nymph StageNewborns ride on the mother's back for 1–2 weeks until first moult; undergo 5–7 moults over 1–3 years to reach maturity
Adult StageAdults can live 3–8 years; males perform a courtship dance (promenade à deux) before mating
Generations per YearSlow-reproducing; typically 1 brood per year
🌾 Orchard Impact
Impact TypeNeutral — does not damage crops; preys on pest insects
BenefitControls populations of pest insects, spiders, and other small invertebrates in and around orchards
Ecosystem RolePredator in the soil and ground-level food web; helps regulate invertebrate populations
🛡️ Pest Status & Safety
Pest ClassificationNot a pest — neutral to beneficial as a predator of pest insects
Danger to HumansMedium — sting is painful but generally not lethal; may cause local swelling, pain, and numbness; allergic reactions possible
Danger to LivestockLow — sting may cause temporary discomfort to small animals
Invasive StatusNative to eastern India (West Bengal and Odisha); not invasive
🚫 Prevention & Control
Personal ProtectionWear closed-toe shoes and gloves when working in areas with debris, rocks, or leaf litter; shake out shoes and clothing before wearing
Habitat ManagementClear debris, woodpiles, and dense leaf litter near living areas; seal gaps in walls and foundations
Night WorkUse a UV blacklight torch when working at night — scorpions fluoresce under UV light and are easy to spot
If StungClean the sting site; apply cold compress; seek medical attention if severe pain, swelling, or allergic reaction occurs
Control NeededGenerally not needed — beneficial predator; relocate rather than kill if found near living areas
