Bleeding Heart

Lamprocapnos spectabilis

Common Names: Bleeding Heart, Old-fashioned Bleeding Heart, Venus's Car, Lyre Flower

Local Name: Bleeding Heart (English)

Family: Papaveraceae

Origin: East Asia (China, Korea, Japan)

Plant Type: Herbaceous perennial

Variety: Pink (classic), Alba (white), Gold Heart (golden foliage), Valentine (red)

Average Lifespan: 3–5 years (individual clumps); indefinite with division

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitClump-forming herbaceous perennial with arching stems; goes dormant in summer heat
Plant Size60–90 cm tall, 45–75 cm spread
LeavesCompound, deeply dissected, fern-like, blue-green to grey-green, 30–40 cm long
FlowersHeart-shaped, pendant, 2–3 cm long, borne in arching racemes of 3–15 flowers
Flower CharacteristicsOuter petals pink or white forming the heart shape; inner petals white, protruding like a drop; zygomorphic symmetry
FruitElongated capsule, 3–4 cm long, splitting open when ripe to release seeds
Fruit ColorGreen maturing to brown
Seed CharacteristicsSmall, round, black, shiny seeds with elaiosomes (ant-dispersed); require cold stratification for germination
Root SystemFleshy, brittle rhizomatous root system; spreads slowly to form clumps
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTemperate; thrives in cool, moist conditions; USDA zones 3–9
Temperature Range10–24°C ideal; tolerates winter cold to −40°C when dormant; goes dormant above 30°C
Rainfall Requirement600–1200 mm annually; consistent moisture preferred
Soil TypeHumus-rich, well-drained, moist loamy soil with high organic matter
Soil pH Preference6.0–7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral)
Sun RequirementPartial shade to full shade; morning sun with afternoon shade ideal
Spacing45–60 cm between plants
Irrigation MethodDrip irrigation or gentle watering at soil level; avoid wetting foliage
Water RequirementModerate; keep soil consistently moist but never waterlogged
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, in large containers (15+ liters) with good drainage; bring indoors in extreme heat
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleOnce in early spring as new growth emerges; light feeding monthly during active growth
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, leaf mold, well-rotted manure, balanced organic fertilizer (10-10-10)
Pesticide FrequencyRarely needed; generally pest-free
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for aphids; slug pellets or beer traps for slugs and snails
Mulching Practice5–8 cm organic mulch (leaf mold, bark chips) to retain moisture and keep roots cool
Training SystemNo training needed; naturally forms graceful arching mounds
Pruning TimeAfter foliage yellows and dies back naturally in summer; cut to ground level in autumn
How to PruneRemove spent flower stems to encourage reblooming; cut back yellowed foliage to ground level
Common PestsAphids, slugs, snails; generally very pest-resistant
Common DiseasesFusarium wilt, verticillium wilt, powdery mildew, stem rot in waterlogged conditions
Disease Prevention & RemediesEnsure good drainage, avoid overhead watering, provide air circulation, remove infected foliage promptly
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsApril–June (spring to early summer); may rebloom in cool autumns
Fruiting SeasonLate spring to early summer; capsules mature 4–6 weeks after flowering
Days to HarvestCut flowers when 3–4 blooms are open on the raceme for best vase life
Harvest IndicatorsFlowers fully colored and pendant; seed capsules turn brown when ripe
Average Yield per Plant10–20 flowering stems per mature clump per season
Pollination TypeInsect-pollinated; primarily by bumblebees
Pollination NotesLong-tongued bumblebees are the primary pollinators; flower shape excludes most other insects
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsBumblebees pry open the heart-shaped petals to access nectar; seeds have elaiosomes for ant dispersal (myrmecochory)
Propagation by SeedsRequires 4–6 weeks cold stratification at 4°C; sow in autumn outdoors or stratify in refrigerator; germination in 30–90 days
Propagation by CuttingsRoot cuttings in late autumn (5–8 cm sections); stem cuttings in spring after new growth emerges
Propagation by GraftingDivision of rhizomes in early spring or autumn is the most reliable method; divide every 3–4 years
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismSymbolizes unconditional love, compassion, and romantic devotion; in Asian folklore, associated with unrequited love and the story of a prince who won a maiden's heart
Traditional UsesCultivated in Chinese and Japanese gardens for centuries as an ornamental; featured in Victorian cottage gardens; popular in shade garden design worldwide
🌿 Medicinal Uses
Traditional MedicineUsed in traditional Chinese medicine for treating bruises, traumatic injuries, and menstrual irregularities
Active CompoundsContains isoquinoline alkaloids including protopine, sanguinarine, and allocryptopine
Anti-inflammatory PropertiesAlkaloid extracts show anti-inflammatory and analgesic activity in traditional preparations
CautionAll parts of the plant are toxic if ingested; can cause skin irritation on contact; keep away from children and pets
