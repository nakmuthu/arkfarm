Nutmeg

Myristica fragrans

Common Names: Nutmeg, Mace (aril), Fragrant Nutmeg

Local Name: Jathikkai (Tamil), Jaiphal (Hindi)

Family: Myristicaceae

Origin: Banda Islands, Indonesia

Plant Type: Evergreen tree, 5–13 m tall

🌿 Botanical Description
Trunk:Smooth, greyish-brown bark exuding a reddish sap (kino) when cut. Dense, spreading canopy with a pyramidal shape when young.
Leaves:Alternate, elliptic-oblong, 5–15 cm long, dark green and glossy above, paler beneath. Aromatic with visible oil glands when held to light.
Flowers:Small, pale yellow, bell-shaped, waxy flowers in axillary clusters. Dioecious — male trees produce clusters of 1–10 flowers; female trees bear solitary or paired flowers.
Fruit:Fleshy, pendulous, pear-shaped drupe, 6–9 cm long, yellow when ripe. Splits open to reveal a glossy dark brown seed (nutmeg) covered by a bright red, lacy aril (mace).
Seed:The seed kernel is the nutmeg spice — ovoid, 2–3 cm long, with a characteristic marbled pattern of dark veins (ruminate endosperm) when cut in cross-section.
🌤️ Growing Conditions
Climate:Tropical; requires consistently warm, humid conditions with no dry season. USDA zones 10–12.
Temperature:20–30 °C; optimal around 25–27 °C. Highly frost-sensitive; growth ceases below 15 °C.
Rainfall:1,500–3,000 mm annually, evenly distributed. High humidity (80%+) is essential for good fruit set.
Altitude:Sea level to 700 m; best performance in lowland tropical areas below 500 m.
Soil:Deep, well-drained, rich volcanic or alluvial loam with pH 5.5–7.0. Requires good organic matter content; intolerant of waterlogging.
Sunlight:Partial shade when young (first 3–4 years); full sun at maturity for maximum fruiting.
🌱 Cultivation & Care
Propagation:Seeds (must be sown fresh; viability lost within 2 weeks). Epicotyl grafting and approach grafting used to ensure female trees and early bearing.
Spacing:8 × 8 m or 9 × 9 m; plant one male tree per 10–20 female trees for wind pollination.
Watering:Regular irrigation during dry spells; young trees are especially sensitive to drought. Mulching conserves soil moisture.
Fertilization:Apply 20 kg organic manure and NPK (500:250:1000 g/tree/year) for bearing trees. Potassium is critical for fruit quality.
Pruning:Minimal pruning needed; remove dead wood and low-hanging branches. Shape young trees to develop a strong central leader.
Pests & Diseases:Fruit rot (Diplodia natalensis) in humid conditions; bark canker; white cedar moth larvae. Shot hole borer can damage trunks of stressed trees.
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering Season:Trees begin flowering at 5–8 years (seed-grown) or 3–4 years (grafted). Flowering occurs year-round in equatorial regions with peaks in June–August.
Pollination:Wind-pollinated; male trees shed abundant pollen. Adequate male-to-female ratio is essential for good fruit set.
Fruit Maturity:Fruits mature 6–9 months after flowering. Ripe fruits split open on the tree, revealing the mace-covered seed.
Yield:A mature tree (15+ years) produces 1,500–2,000 fruits per year. Peak production at 25–30 years; trees can bear for over 60 years.
Processing:Mace is carefully separated from the seed and flattened for drying. Nutmeg seeds are dried slowly over 6–8 weeks until the kernel rattles inside the shell.
💰 Economic Importance
Dual Spice:Unique among spice plants — produces two distinct spices from one fruit: nutmeg (seed kernel) and mace (aril). Both command high market prices.
Production:Indonesia (Banda Islands, North Maluku) produces 75% of the world's nutmeg. India (Kerala), Grenada, and Sri Lanka are other major producers.
Essential Oil:Nutmeg oil (myristicin, sabinene, α-pinene) used in food flavoring, perfumery, and pharmaceutical preparations. Nutmeg butter extracted for cosmetics.
Culinary Use:Essential in béchamel sauce, eggnog, pumpkin pie spice, garam masala, and Dutch/Indonesian cuisine. Mace is used in lighter dishes, sauces, and baking.
💊 Medicinal Uses
Digestive Aid:Used in traditional medicine to treat diarrhea, nausea, and stomach cramps. Small doses stimulate appetite and improve digestion.
Sleep Aid:Warm milk with a pinch of nutmeg is a traditional remedy for insomnia. Contains trimyristin, which has mild sedative properties.
Pain Relief:Nutmeg oil applied topically relieves muscle and joint pain. Used in traditional Unani medicine as an analgesic and anti-inflammatory.
Caution:Nutmeg contains myristicin, which in large doses (more than 5 g) can cause hallucinations, nausea, and toxicity. Normal culinary amounts are safe.
