Clove

Syzygium aromaticum

Common Names: Clove, Clove Tree

Local Name: Kirambu (Tamil), Laung (Hindi)

Family: Myrtaceae

Origin: Maluku Islands, Indonesia

Plant Type: Evergreen tree, 8–12 m tall

🌿 Botanical Description
Trunk:Smooth grey bark with a straight trunk; branches begin low on the tree forming a conical canopy.
Leaves:Large, opposite, simple, ovate-lanceolate, 8–13 cm long, glossy dark green with prominent oil glands visible when held to light.
Flowers:Crimson flowers borne in terminal clusters (cymes) of 3–15. Each flower has a long calyx tube topped by four spreading sepals and four unopened petals forming the characteristic bud.
Fruit:A fleshy drupe called "mother of clove," about 2.5 cm long, dark purple when ripe, containing one or two seeds.
Root System:Deep taproot with extensive lateral roots; sensitive to waterlogging.
🌤️ Growing Conditions
Climate:Tropical maritime climate; thrives in warm, humid conditions with no prolonged dry season.
Temperature:20–30 °C; cannot tolerate frost or temperatures below 15 °C.
Rainfall:1,500–2,500 mm annually, well-distributed throughout the year.
Altitude:Sea level to 900 m; best performance below 600 m.
Soil:Rich, loamy, well-drained volcanic soils with pH 5.5–6.5; does not tolerate waterlogged or sandy soils.
Sunlight:Partial shade when young; full sun at maturity for best bud production.
🌱 Cultivation & Care
Propagation:Seeds (must be sown fresh within one week of harvest as viability drops rapidly). Vegetative propagation through air-layering is also practiced.
Spacing:6–7 m between trees in all directions.
Watering:Regular watering during establishment; mature trees rely on rainfall but benefit from irrigation during dry spells.
Fertilization:Apply organic manure (10–15 kg/tree/year) and NPK fertilizer; increase potassium during bud formation.
Pruning:Minimal pruning; remove dead or crossing branches to maintain airflow within the canopy.
Pests & Diseases:Stem borers, scale insects; Sumatra disease (bacterial wilt caused by Ralstonia syzygii) is the most serious threat.
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering Season:Trees begin flowering after 5–7 years; main flowering occurs September–October in the Northern Hemisphere.
Harvest Stage:Buds are harvested when they turn pink but before they open. Timing is critical — open flowers have significantly less eugenol.
Yield:A mature tree (15+ years) can produce 3–7 kg of dried cloves per year; peak production at 20–25 years.
Drying:Harvested buds are sun-dried for 4–5 days until they turn dark brown and the moisture content drops below 12%.
💰 Economic Importance
Spice Trade:One of the most valuable spices historically; Indonesia, Madagascar, and Tanzania are the top producers.
Essential Oil:Clove oil (70–90% eugenol) is extracted from buds, stems, and leaves; used in dentistry, food flavoring, and perfumery.
Kretek Industry:Indonesia consumes over 50% of world clove production for kretek (clove cigarettes).
Food Industry:Used in spice blends (garam masala, Chinese five-spice), baked goods, beverages, and meat preservation.
🏛️ Cultural Significance
Historical Trade:Cloves were among the first spices traded internationally; Arab traders brought them to Europe by the 4th century. The spice trade wars of the 16th–17th centuries were largely driven by cloves.
Traditional Use:In Indian culture, cloves are offered in religious ceremonies and chewed as a mouth freshener after meals. In Chinese tradition, courtiers held cloves in their mouths when addressing the emperor.
Symbolism:Represents dignity and warmth in the language of spices; associated with protection and prosperity in many Asian cultures.
💊 Medicinal Uses
Dental Care:Eugenol is a powerful analgesic and antiseptic; clove oil is widely used for toothache relief and in dental cements.
Digestive Aid:Stimulates digestive enzyme secretion; used in traditional medicine to treat flatulence, indigestion, and nausea.
Antimicrobial:Strong antibacterial and antifungal properties; effective against foodborne pathogens and oral bacteria.
Anti-inflammatory:Eugenol exhibits anti-inflammatory activity comparable to some pharmaceutical agents; used in Ayurveda for joint pain.
