Giant Taro

Alocasia macrorrhizos

Common Names: Giant Taro, Upright Elephant Ear, Giant Alocasia, Ape

Local Name: Cheppankizhangu (Tamil), Arbi (Hindi), Chembu (Malayalam)

Family: Araceae

Origin: Tropical Asia and Pacific Islands

Plant Type: Perennial tropical herb with large rhizome

Average Lifespan: Many years; spreads by offsets

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitUpright, clump-forming herb with massive leaves on tall petioles
Plant Size2–5 meters tall; leaves up to 1.8 meters long
LeavesEnormous, arrow-shaped, glossy dark green; held upright on long petioles; prominent veins
FlowersSpathe and spadix inflorescence; cream-white; fragrant; rarely produced in cultivation
Root SystemLarge, starchy rhizome; produces offsets (pups) around base
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical and subtropical; warm and humid
Temperature Range18–35°C; frost-sensitive
Soil TypeRich, moist, well-drained soil with high organic matter
Soil pH Preference5.5–7.0
Sun RequirementPartial shade to bright indirect light; protect from harsh afternoon sun
Water RequirementHigh; keep soil consistently moist; tolerates wet conditions
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, in very large containers (50+ liters)
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleMonthly during growing season with balanced fertilizer
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, well-rotted manure, worm castings
Common PestsSpider mites, mealybugs, aphids, taro beetle
Common DiseasesRoot rot, leaf blight, taro leaf blight (Phytophthora)
Pruning TimeRemove dead or damaged leaves at base; divide clumps when overcrowded
How to PruneCut leaf stems at base; wear gloves — sap contains calcium oxalate crystals which irritate skin
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Propagation by CuttingsDivision of offsets (pups) from base of plant; most reliable method
Propagation by SeedsRarely used; seeds germinate slowly
💰 Economic Importance
Market UsesOrnamental garden plant, indoor statement plant, tropical landscape
Market Value (Approx.)₹200–800 per plant depending on size
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismSacred plant in Pacific Island cultures; rhizome is a traditional food staple in many tropical regions
Traditional UsesRhizome eaten after thorough cooking (raw is toxic); leaves used as natural plates; widely grown as ornamental in tropical gardens
