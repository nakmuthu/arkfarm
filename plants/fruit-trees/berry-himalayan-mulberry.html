Himalayan Mulberry

Morus serrata

Common Names: Himalayan Mulberry, Mountain Mulberry, Indian Mulberry

Local Name: Kimu / Tut (Hindi), Himshahtoot (colloquial)

Family: Moraceae

Origin: Himalayan region (India, Nepal, Pakistan, Afghanistan)

Plant Type: Perennial deciduous tree

Variety: Wild Himalayan species — dark purple, intensely flavored fruit

Average Lifespan: 60–100+ years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitLarge deciduous tree with broad, spreading crown
Plant Size10–20 meters tall; canopy spread 8–15 meters
LeavesAlternate, ovate to cordate, 8–20 cm long; deeply serrated margins; rough texture
FlowersGreenish catkins, 3–5 cm long; appear before or with new leaves
Flower CharacteristicsDioecious (separate male and female trees); wind-pollinated
FruitCylindrical syncarp, 2–4 cm long; juicy, sweet-tart flavor
Fruit ColorDark purple to black when fully ripe
Seed CharacteristicsTiny seeds within drupelets; barely noticeable
Root SystemDeep, extensive root system adapted to mountain slopes
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTemperate to cool subtropical; adapted to mountain conditions
Temperature Range10–30°C ideal; tolerates -15°C when dormant; very cold-hardy
Rainfall Requirement800–2000 mm annually; tolerates seasonal drought
Soil TypeWell-drained, rocky to loamy mountain soils
Soil pH Preference5.5–7.5
Sun RequirementFull sun to partial shade (5+ hours daily)
Spacing8–12 meters between trees
Irrigation MethodRain-fed in natural habitat; supplemental irrigation in dry periods
Water RequirementLow to moderate; adapted to mountain rainfall patterns
Can it Grow in Pots?Not ideal due to large size; young trees possible in very large containers
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer Schedule1–2 times per year; spring application sufficient
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, FYM, leaf mold from forest floor
Pesticide FrequencyRarely needed; naturally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil if needed; generally not required
Mulching PracticeNatural leaf litter provides mulch; additional organic mulch beneficial
Training SystemNatural form; minimal training needed
Pruning TimeLate winter while dormant
How to PruneRemove dead and damaged branches; thin for light penetration
Common PestsGenerally pest-free; occasional scale insects or caterpillars
Common DiseasesLeaf spot, canker in stressed trees
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood site selection, avoid waterlogging, remove infected branches
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsMarch–April (Himalayan spring)
Fruiting SeasonMay–July; ripens progressively
Days to Harvest40–60 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit turns dark purple-black, softens, and drops when ripe
Average Yield per Plant30–80 kg per tree (mature)
Pollination TypeWind pollination
Pollination NotesDioecious — needs male tree within wind range for fruit production
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsStrictly dioecious; male trees produce abundant wind-borne pollen
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate in 3–6 weeks; cold stratification improves germination
Propagation by CuttingsHardwood cuttings in winter; moderate success rate
Propagation by GraftingCleft grafting on Morus alba rootstock for faster fruiting
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageLow; adapted to rain-fed mountain conditions
Soil Conservation PracticesExcellent for slope stabilization; deep roots prevent landslides
Organic Practices UsedNaturally low-input tree; no chemicals needed
Companion PlantsMountain herbs, wild grasses, leguminous shrubs
Biodiversity BenefitsCritical food source for Himalayan birds and wildlife; supports forest ecosystem
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsHimalayan foothills of India, Nepal, Pakistan
Market UsesFresh fruit (local markets), timber, fodder
Processing UsesDried fruit, jam, local wine, timber for furniture and tools
Market Value (Approx.)₹80–200 per kg (seasonal, local markets)
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismRevered as a sacred tree in Himalayan communities; symbol of mountain resilience
Traditional UsesFruit eaten fresh and dried for winter; wood used for agricultural tools; bark for rope-making
