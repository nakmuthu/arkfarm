Strawberry Guava

Psidium cattleianum

Common Names: Strawberry Guava, Cattley Guava, Cherry Guava

Local Name: Strawberry Koiya (Tamil), Lal Amrood (Hindi)

Family: Myrtaceae

Origin: Eastern Brazil (Atlantic coastal forests)

Plant Type: Perennial evergreen shrub or small tree

Variety: Red Strawberry Guava (var. cattleianum) — small, deep red fruit

Average Lifespan: 30–40 years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitDense, multi-stemmed shrub or small tree with smooth bark
Plant Size2–6 meters tall; compact canopy
LeavesSimple, opposite, obovate, 5–10 cm long; thick, glossy dark green, leathery
FlowersWhite, solitary, 2–3 cm diameter with numerous stamens
Flower CharacteristicsFragrant, 4–5 white petals with prominent brush-like stamens; self-fertile
FruitSmall, round, 2–4 cm diameter; deep red to maroon skin with white to pinkish pulp
Fruit ColorDeep red to dark maroon when ripe
Seed CharacteristicsSeveral small, hard seeds embedded in soft pulp
Root SystemShallow to moderately deep fibrous root system
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical to subtropical; more cold-tolerant than common guava
Temperature Range15–30°C ideal; tolerates brief frost down to -5°C
Rainfall Requirement1000–2000 mm annually; tolerates moderate drought
Soil TypeAdaptable; prefers well-drained sandy loam to clay loam
Soil pH Preference5.0–7.0
Sun RequirementFull sun to partial shade (4–8 hours daily)
Spacing3–5 meters between plants
Irrigation MethodDrip or basin irrigation; consistent moisture during fruiting
Water RequirementModerate; drought-tolerant once established
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, excellent container plant in 20–30 liter pots
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer Schedule3–4 times per year during growing season
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, well-rotted manure, fish emulsion, bone meal
Pesticide FrequencyMinimal; generally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for scale insects; fruit fly traps
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch (leaf litter, wood chips) around base
Training SystemOpen center or natural form; can be hedged
Pruning TimeAfter harvest; light pruning year-round
How to PruneRemove suckers, thin interior for airflow; tip-prune for bushier growth
Common PestsFruit fly, scale insects, guava moth
Common DiseasesAnthracnose, algal leaf spot, root rot in waterlogged soil
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood drainage, avoid overhead watering, copper-based fungicides
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsSpring to early summer (March–May in tropics)
Fruiting SeasonSummer to autumn; 90–150 days after flowering
Days to Harvest90–150 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit turns deep red, softens slightly, and develops sweet aroma
Average Yield per Plant10–20 kg per year (mature tree)
Pollination TypeSelf-pollination; also insect-pollinated
Pollination NotesSelf-fertile; bees improve fruit set and size
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsSelf-compatible; single tree can produce fruit; bees enhance yield
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate in 4–8 weeks; produces true-to-type plants
Propagation by CuttingsSemi-hardwood cuttings possible but slow to root; use rooting hormone
Propagation by GraftingAir layering or veneer grafting for faster fruiting
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageLow to moderate; drought-tolerant once established
Soil Conservation PracticesDense root system stabilizes soil; good for erosion control
Organic Practices UsedMulching, composting, minimal chemical inputs needed
Companion PlantsLeguminous ground covers, banana, sweet potato
Biodiversity BenefitsFruit attracts birds and wildlife; flowers support pollinators
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsBrazil, Hawaii, Florida, Southeast Asia, India
Market UsesFresh fruit, juice, jams, jellies
Processing UsesJam, jelly, fruit paste, beverages, ice cream flavoring
Market Value (Approx.)₹100–250 per kg (specialty fruit)
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismRepresents tropical abundance; valued as a dooryard fruit tree
Traditional UsesFruit eaten fresh; leaves used in traditional herbal teas; bark used in folk medicine
