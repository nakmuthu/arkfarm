Mulberry

Morus alba

Common Names: Mulberry, White Mulberry, Shahtoot

Local Name: Mucukkattai (Tamil), Shahtoot (Hindi), Tuti (Kannada)

Family: Moraceae

Origin: China; widely cultivated across Asia

Plant Type: Perennial deciduous tree

Variety: Standard white mulberry — sweet, mild-flavored fruit

Average Lifespan: 50–75 years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitFast-growing deciduous tree with spreading, rounded crown
Plant Size10–15 meters tall; canopy spread 8–12 meters
LeavesAlternate, simple, ovate to lobed, 5–15 cm long; serrated margins; variable shape
FlowersInconspicuous catkins; greenish-white, 1–3 cm long
Flower CharacteristicsDioecious or monoecious; wind-pollinated; catkins appear with new leaves
FruitMultiple fruit (syncarp), 1–3 cm long; composed of many small drupes
Fruit ColorWhite to pinkish-lavender when ripe (white mulberry)
Seed CharacteristicsTiny seeds within each drupelet; barely noticeable
Root SystemDeep taproot with extensive lateral roots; can be invasive
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTemperate to subtropical; very adaptable
Temperature Range15–35°C ideal; tolerates -10°C when dormant
Rainfall Requirement600–2500 mm annually; drought-tolerant once established
Soil TypeHighly adaptable; prefers deep, well-drained loamy soil
Soil pH Preference6.0–7.5
Sun RequirementFull sun (6+ hours daily)
Spacing6–10 meters between trees; closer for hedge/sericulture
Irrigation MethodBasin or drip irrigation; minimal once established
Water RequirementLow to moderate; very drought-tolerant
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, dwarf varieties in 30–50 liter pots
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer Schedule2–3 times per year; spring and post-harvest
Recommended Organic FertilizersFYM, compost, neem cake, vermicompost
Pesticide FrequencyMinimal; generally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for scale or mealybugs if needed
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch around base; leaf litter serves as natural mulch
Training SystemCentral leader or open center; can be heavily pruned for sericulture
Pruning TimeLate winter while dormant; or after harvest
How to PruneShape as desired; remove dead wood; can be coppiced for leaf production
Common PestsScale insects, whiteflies, mealybugs, silkworm (beneficial in sericulture)
Common DiseasesLeaf spot, powdery mildew, root rot, bacterial blight
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood air circulation, avoid waterlogging, remove infected parts
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsFebruary–April (spring)
Fruiting SeasonApril–June; fruits ripen over several weeks
Days to Harvest30–45 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit fully colored, soft, and drops easily when branch is shaken
Average Yield per Plant15–50 kg per tree (mature)
Pollination TypeWind pollination primarily
Pollination NotesSome varieties monoecious (self-fertile); dioecious types need male tree nearby
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsWind-pollinated; pollen released in explosive puffs from male catkins
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate easily in 2–4 weeks; not true-to-type
Propagation by CuttingsHardwood cuttings (20–30 cm) in winter; root easily in 4–6 weeks
Propagation by GraftingWhip grafting or cleft grafting on seedling rootstock for named varieties
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageLow; very drought-tolerant once established
Soil Conservation PracticesDeep roots prevent erosion; deciduous leaves enrich soil
Organic Practices UsedMinimal inputs needed; leaf litter composting
Companion PlantsLeguminous cover crops, herbs, shade-tolerant vegetables
Biodiversity BenefitsFruit attracts birds and wildlife; supports silk industry; provides shade
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsChina, India (Karnataka, Tamil Nadu), Turkey, Iran, USA
Market UsesFresh fruit, dried fruit, sericulture (silk production)
Processing UsesJam, juice, wine, dried mulberries, syrup, tea (leaves)
Market Value (Approx.)₹100–300 per kg (fresh); leaves valuable for sericulture
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismSymbol of patience and wisdom in Chinese culture; associated with silk production for millennia
Traditional UsesLeaves feed silkworms; fruit in traditional medicine; bark used for paper-making in Asia

🌿 Medicinal Uses
Anxiety & SleepNot a primary use; leaf tea used in traditional Chinese medicine for calming
