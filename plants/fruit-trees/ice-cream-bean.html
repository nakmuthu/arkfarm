Ice Cream Bean

Inga edulis

Common Names: Ice Cream Bean, Guaba, Guama, Joaquiniquil, Pacay

Local Name: Guaba (Spanish), Ingá-cipó (Portuguese)

Family: Fabaceae

Origin: Central and South America (Amazon Basin to Central America)

Plant Type: Perennial evergreen tree

Variety: Several Inga species cultivated; I. edulis most common

Average Lifespan: 30–50 years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitFast-growing evergreen tree with broad spreading canopy
Plant Size10–30 meters tall; wide spreading crown up to 15 meters
LeavesPinnately compound, 4–6 pairs of leaflets with winged rachis, 20–30 cm long
FlowersWhite, tubular, with prominent stamens, borne in axillary spikes
Flower CharacteristicsFragrant; numerous white stamens give brush-like appearance; attract hummingbirds and bees
FruitVery long cylindrical pod, 30–100 cm long, containing seeds embedded in sweet white cottony pulp
Fruit ColorGreen pod; white cotton-like sweet pulp inside
Seed CharacteristicsLarge black seeds, 2–3 cm, embedded in sweet aril; recalcitrant
Root SystemDeep taproot with extensive lateral roots; nitrogen-fixing nodules
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical lowland to mid-elevation; frost-intolerant
Temperature Range20–35°C ideal; does not tolerate frost
Rainfall Requirement1500–3000 mm annually; tolerates wet conditions
Soil TypeAdaptable; grows in poor acidic soils; prefers well-drained loam
Soil pH Preference4.5–7.0 (very adaptable)
Sun RequirementFull sun; fast growth in open conditions
Spacing8–12 meters between trees
Irrigation MethodRain-fed in tropics; supplemental watering in dry periods
Water RequirementModerate; tolerates both wet and moderately dry conditions
Can it Grow in Pots?Not recommended — very fast-growing and large
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleMinimal; nitrogen-fixing roots reduce fertilizer needs
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, phosphorus supplements; nitrogen rarely needed
Pesticide FrequencyRarely needed; naturally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil if needed
Mulching PracticeSelf-mulching from abundant leaf litter; excellent for soil improvement
Training SystemNatural spreading form; used as shade tree in agroforestry
Pruning TimeRegular pollarding for agroforestry; after fruiting for fruit production
How to PrunePollard or coppice for mulch production; light pruning for fruit trees
Common PestsPod borers, psyllids (minimal impact)
Common DiseasesGenerally disease-free; occasional leaf spot
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood air circulation; rarely needs treatment
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsYear-round in tropics; peaks during rainy season
Fruiting SeasonYear-round with peaks; pods mature 3–4 months after flowering
Days to Harvest90–120 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsPods become plump, slightly soft; green colour may lighten
Average Yield per Plant50–100 kg of pods per tree per year
Pollination TypeInsect and hummingbird pollination
Pollination NotesHummingbirds, bees, and moths are primary pollinators; night-blooming attracts moths
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsCross-pollination by hummingbirds and insects; some self-fertility
Propagation by SeedsFresh seeds germinate in 1–2 weeks; must be sown immediately (recalcitrant); very fast growth
Propagation by CuttingsLarge hardwood cuttings possible; air layering also works
Propagation by GraftingNot commonly grafted; seedlings grow fast and fruit in 3–4 years
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageModerate; adapted to tropical rainfall
Soil Conservation PracticesExceptional nitrogen fixer; improves degraded soils; heavy leaf litter builds humus
Organic Practices UsedKey agroforestry species; provides shade, mulch, and nitrogen to companion crops
Companion PlantsCacao, coffee, vanilla — classic agroforestry shade tree
Biodiversity BenefitsAttracts hummingbirds, bees, moths; provides habitat; improves soil for entire ecosystem
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsPeru, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador, Central America
Market UsesFresh fruit (street food), shade tree, firewood, timber
Processing UsesEaten fresh; pulp used in beverages; wood for fuel and construction
Market Value (Approx.)$1–3 per pod (local markets); valued more for agroforestry services
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismSymbol of sustainable agroforestry; important in Amazonian food culture
Traditional UsesSweet pulp eaten fresh as snack; widely sold in Latin American markets; shade tree for cacao and coffee
