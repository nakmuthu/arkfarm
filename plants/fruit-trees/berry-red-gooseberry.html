Red Gooseberry / Amla

Phyllanthus emblica

Common Names: Indian Gooseberry, Amla, Amalaki, Emblic Myrobalan

Local Name: Nellikai (Tamil), Amla (Hindi), Nellikka (Malayalam)

Family: Phyllanthaceae

Origin: Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia

Plant Type: Perennial deciduous tree

Variety: Banarasi, Chakaiya, Francis, NA-7, Krishna — various improved cultivars

Average Lifespan: 50–100+ years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitMedium to large deciduous tree with spreading, drooping branches
Plant Size8–18 meters tall; canopy spread 6–10 meters
LeavesSmall, linear-oblong, 1–2 cm long; closely set on branchlets resembling pinnate leaves
FlowersTiny, greenish-yellow, in dense clusters along branchlets
Flower CharacteristicsMonoecious; male and female flowers on same tree; inconspicuous
FruitRound, 2–4 cm diameter; smooth, hard, with 6 vertical furrows; fibrous, sour-astringent flesh
Fruit ColorLight green to yellowish-green; some varieties with reddish tinge
Seed CharacteristicsHard, 6-ridged stone containing 6 seeds
Root SystemDeep taproot with extensive lateral roots; drought-adapted
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical to subtropical; tolerates semi-arid conditions
Temperature Range20–40°C ideal; tolerates up to 46°C; light frost tolerance
Rainfall Requirement500–2500 mm annually; very drought-tolerant
Soil TypeHighly adaptable; grows in sandy, loamy, rocky, and even saline soils
Soil pH Preference5.0–8.0 (very wide range)
Sun RequirementFull sun (6+ hours daily)
Spacing8–10 meters between trees
Irrigation MethodBasin irrigation; minimal once established
Water RequirementLow; extremely drought-tolerant once established
Can it Grow in Pots?Young trees in large pots (40+ liters); will limit size
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer Schedule2–3 times per year; pre-monsoon and post-harvest
Recommended Organic FertilizersFYM (20–30 kg/tree/year), neem cake, vermicompost, bone meal
Pesticide FrequencyMinimal; generally hardy
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for bark-eating caterpillar; pheromone traps for fruit borer
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch around base; leaf litter serves as natural mulch
Training SystemModified central leader; open center for mature trees
Pruning TimeAfter harvest (March–April); remove dead wood
How to PruneRemove dead, diseased, and crossing branches; thin canopy for light
Common PestsBark-eating caterpillar, shoot gall maker, fruit borer, mealy bug
Common DiseasesRust, fruit rot, anthracnose, leaf spot
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood sanitation, copper fungicides, remove fallen fruit, proper spacing
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsMarch–May (India)
Fruiting SeasonOctober–February; fruits mature in winter
Days to Harvest180–240 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit turns yellowish-green, becomes slightly soft; fibrous texture
Average Yield per Plant50–200 kg per tree (mature); increases with age
Pollination TypeCross-pollination by insects and wind
Pollination NotesPlant 2+ varieties for better fruit set; bees assist pollination
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsMonoecious; cross-pollination improves yield; insects and wind both contribute
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate in 2–4 weeks; seedlings take 8–10 years to fruit
Propagation by CuttingsDifficult; air layering possible but not common
Propagation by GraftingSoftwood grafting or budding on seedling rootstock (preferred); fruits in 3–4 years
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageVery low; one of the most drought-tolerant fruit trees
Soil Conservation PracticesDeep roots prevent erosion; grows on degraded and marginal lands
Organic Practices UsedNaturally low-input tree; panchagavya, jeevamrutha
Companion PlantsTurmeric, ginger, legumes as intercrops; compatible with agroforestry
Biodiversity BenefitsProvides food for birds and wildlife; supports forest ecosystem restoration
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsIndia (UP, Rajasthan, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu), Nepal, Sri Lanka
Market UsesFresh fruit, Ayurvedic medicine, hair oil, health supplements
Processing UsesAmla candy, pickle, juice, powder, Chyawanprash, hair oil, triphala
Market Value (Approx.)₹30–80 per kg (fresh); processed products much higher value
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismSacred tree in Hinduism; associated with Lord Vishnu; Amla Navami festival celebrates the tree
Traditional UsesCentral to Ayurvedic medicine for millennia; key ingredient in Chyawanprash and Triphala; hair care
🍈 Nutritional Information
Calories44 kcal per 100g
Dietary Fiber4.3 g per 100g
Vitamin C600–900 mg per 100g (one of the highest natural sources)
Vitamin A290 IU per 100g
Iron0.31 mg per 100g
Potassium198 mg per 100g
AntioxidantsExtremely high in tannins, gallic acid, ellagic acid, superoxide dismutase
Other Key NutrientsCalcium, phosphorus, chromium, zinc
🌿 Medicinal Uses
Anxiety & SleepAdaptogenic properties help manage stress; used in Ayurvedic rasayanas
Immune SupportExceptionally high vitamin C; key ingredient in Chyawanprash for immunity
Anti-inflammatory PropertiesTannins and gallic acid show potent anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects
Heart HealthMay reduce cholesterol and blood pressure; supports arterial health
Digestive HealthKey component of Triphala for digestive health; fiber aids regularity
