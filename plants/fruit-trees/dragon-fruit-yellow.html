Dragon Fruit - Yellow

Selenicereus megalanthus

Common Names: Yellow Dragon Fruit, Yellow Pitaya, Colombian Pitaya, Yellow Pitahaya

Local Name: Manja Dragon Pazham (Tamil), Yellow Dragon Fruit (Hindi/English)

Family: Cactaceae

Origin: Colombia, Ecuador, Bolivia, Peru (South America)

Plant Type: Perennial epiphytic cactus vine; climbing with aerial roots

Variety: Yellow-skinned with white flesh; considered the sweetest dragon fruit variety

Average Lifespan: 20–30+ years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitClimbing epiphytic cactus; triangular, ribbed stems with prominent spines; climbs using aerial roots
Plant Size3–8 meters long; trained on posts at 1.5–2 meters height
LeavesNo true leaves; photosynthesis in green triangular stems; more spiny than Hylocereus species
FlowersLarge white flowers; 20–30 cm across; fragrant; open only at night
Flower CharacteristicsNight-blooming; pollinated by moths and bats; self-fertile; hand pollination improves yield
FruitRound to oval; 8–12 cm; bright yellow skin with prominent spines (no scales); white flesh
Fruit ColorBright yellow skin; white flesh with tiny black seeds; sweeter and more aromatic than red varieties
Seed CharacteristicsNumerous tiny black seeds throughout the flesh; edible; 1–2 mm
Root SystemShallow fibrous roots plus aerial roots; drought-tolerant
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical and subtropical; slightly more cold-tolerant than Hylocereus species
Temperature Range15–38°C; tolerates slightly cooler temperatures than red dragon fruit
Rainfall Requirement500–1500 mm; drought-tolerant; excess moisture causes root rot
Soil TypeWell-drained sandy loam; high organic matter; excellent drainage essential
Soil pH Preference6.0–7.0
Sun RequirementFull sun (6+ hours); tolerates partial shade
Spacing3–4 meters between posts; 2–3 plants per post
Irrigation MethodDrip irrigation; water deeply but infrequently; avoid waterlogging
Water RequirementLow to moderate; drought-tolerant; overwatering causes root rot
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, in large pots (40–60 liters) with post support
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleMonthly; balanced NPK; high potassium during fruiting
Recommended Organic FertilizersVermicompost, neem cake, bone meal, wood ash
Pesticide FrequencyAs needed; generally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for mealybugs and scale; copper fungicide for stem rot
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch around base; helps retain moisture
Training SystemTrain on concrete or wooden posts (1.5–2 m tall); allow stems to cascade over top
Pruning TimeAfter harvest; remove old, unproductive stems
How to PruneRemove stems older than 3 years; keep 4–6 productive stems per post; wear gloves (spiny)
Common PestsMealybugs, scale insects, ants
Common DiseasesStem rot (Fusarium), anthracnose, bacterial soft rot
Disease Prevention & RemediesExcellent drainage; avoid overwatering; copper fungicide; remove infected stems
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsJune–November; multiple flushes; flowers open at night
Fruiting SeasonJuly–December; fewer but larger fruits than red varieties
Days to Harvest35–45 days from flowering to ripe fruit (longer than red varieties)
Harvest IndicatorsSkin turns bright yellow; spines dry and brown; fruit gives slightly when pressed; sweet aroma
Average Yield per Plant15–30 kg per post per year (mature); fewer but larger fruits
Pollination TypeNight-pollinated by moths and bats; self-fertile
Pollination NotesSelf-fertile; hand pollination at night improves fruit set; more self-fertile than Hylocereus species
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsNight-blooming; moth and bat pollinated; self-fertile; hand pollination improves yield
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate in 2–3 weeks; slow to fruit (3–5 years)
Propagation by CuttingsStem cuttings (30–40 cm) root easily; preferred method; fruits in 1–2 years; dry cut end before planting
Propagation by GraftingGrafting on Hylocereus undatus rootstock improves vigor and yield
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageLow; drought-tolerant cactus
Soil Conservation PracticesGrows on poor soils; minimal soil disturbance needed
Organic Practices UsedVermicompost-based feeding; minimal chemical inputs
Companion PlantsLeguminous cover crops; shade-tolerant plants between posts
Biodiversity BenefitsNight flowers attract moths and bats; supports nocturnal pollinator populations
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsColombia, Ecuador, Israel, India (limited); premium market worldwide
Market UsesPremium fresh fruit; health food; gourmet market; commands highest price of all dragon fruit varieties
Processing UsesJuice, smoothies, ice cream, health supplements
Market Value (Approx.)₹200–500 per kg; premium variety; cuttings ₹100–300 each
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismConsidered the most prized dragon fruit variety; symbol of exotic luxury in Asian markets
Traditional UsesFruit used for digestive health; flowers used in traditional medicine in South America
