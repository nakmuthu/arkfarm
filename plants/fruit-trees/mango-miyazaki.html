Mango Miyazaki

Mangifera indica 'Miyazaki' (Irwin)

Common Names: Miyazaki Mango, Taiyo no Tamago (Egg of the Sun), Japanese Red Mango, Irwin Mango

Local Name: 太陽のタマゴ (Japanese), Miyazaki Mango (English)

Family: Anacardiaceae

Origin: Irwin variety from Florida, USA; perfected in Miyazaki Prefecture, Japan

Plant Type: Perennial evergreen tree

Variety: Irwin cultivar — deep ruby-red, premium greenhouse mango; world's most expensive mango variety

Average Lifespan: 30–60+ years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitCompact, semi-dwarf tree with dense, rounded canopy; well-suited to greenhouse cultivation
Plant Size3–6 meters tall (managed); canopy spread 3–5 meters
LeavesSimple, alternate, lanceolate, 15–25 cm long; new flush deep burgundy-red turning glossy dark green
FlowersSmall, pinkish-red in compact terminal panicles (15–25 cm long)
Flower CharacteristicsBoth male and bisexual flowers; sweet fragrance; blooms prolifically under controlled conditions
FruitOval to ovate, 300–500 g (premium grade 350g+); thin skin with rich, creamy, fiberless pulp; Brix 15–18°
Fruit ColorDeep ruby-red to crimson over entire surface; no green patches at maturity
Seed CharacteristicsMedium monoembryonic seed; relatively small compared to fruit size
Root SystemModerate taproot with lateral roots; manageable in container or greenhouse settings
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical to warm subtropical; commercially grown in heated greenhouses in Japan
Temperature Range25–32°C ideal; greenhouse maintained at 28–30°C; frost-sensitive below 5°C
Rainfall RequirementControlled irrigation in greenhouse; 800–1500 mm equivalent annually
Soil TypeWell-drained, fertile loamy soil; premium potting mix in greenhouse culture
Soil pH Preference5.5–7.0
Sun RequirementFull sun (8+ hours); supplemental lighting in greenhouse during winter
Spacing3–5 meters in greenhouse; 6–8 meters in open field (tropical regions)
Irrigation MethodPrecision drip irrigation; carefully controlled moisture during fruit development
Water RequirementModerate; precise water management critical for premium fruit quality
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, excellent container variety; commonly grown in large pots in Japanese greenhouses
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleMonthly during growing season; balanced NPK with extra potassium during fruiting
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, fish emulsion, bone meal, seaweed extract, vermicompost
Pesticide FrequencyMinimal in greenhouse; preventive sprays 2–3 times per season
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil, insecticidal soap, biological controls (predatory mites), sticky traps
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch in greenhouse beds; reflective mulch to enhance red coloring
Training SystemEspalier or open vase; individual fruit suspended in nets to prevent drop damage
Pruning TimeAfter harvest; maintain compact form for greenhouse management
How to PruneTip-prune for compact growth; thin fruit to 1–2 per panicle for premium size
Common PestsThrips, mealybug, scale insects, spider mites (greenhouse pests)
Common DiseasesAnthracnose, powdery mildew, stem-end rot
Disease Prevention & RemediesGreenhouse climate control, good ventilation, copper fungicides, fruit bagging
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsFebruary–March (Japan greenhouse); varies in tropical open-field
Fruiting SeasonApril–July (Japan); year-round possible in tropics
Days to Harvest100–120 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit turns uniformly deep red, drops naturally into net, intense sweet aroma; Brix 15+
Average Yield per Plant20–50 premium fruits per tree (thinned for quality); up to 100 in open field
Pollination TypeSelf-fertile; cross-pollination improves fruit set
Pollination NotesHand pollination practiced in greenhouses; bees introduced during flowering
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsSelf-fertile Irwin variety; hand pollination and bee introduction in greenhouse
Propagation by SeedsMonoembryonic; seedlings do not come true-to-type; used for rootstock
Propagation by CuttingsAir layering possible but grafting preferred for uniformity
Propagation by GraftingVeneer or cleft grafting on vigorous rootstock; tissue culture also used in Japan
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageModerate; precision irrigation minimizes waste in greenhouse
Soil Conservation PracticesGreenhouse cultivation reduces land footprint; mulching conserves moisture
Organic Practices UsedIntegrated pest management, biological controls, organic fertilizers
Companion PlantsNot typically intercropped in greenhouse; herbs as pest deterrents in open field
Biodiversity BenefitsGreenhouse pollinator programs support bee populations; open-field trees provide habitat
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsMiyazaki Prefecture (Japan), Okinawa, Taiwan, India (emerging), Thailand
Market UsesUltra-premium gift fruit, luxury dessert, high-end retail
Processing UsesRarely processed; consumed fresh as luxury fruit; some use in premium confections
Market Value (Approx.)¥3,000–5,000 per fruit ($20–35 USD); Taiyo no Tamago grade: ¥10,000–50,000+ per pair at auction
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismTaiyo no Tamago (Egg of the Sun) — symbol of Japanese agricultural craftsmanship and luxury gifting culture
Traditional UsesPremium gift fruit in Japanese oseibo and ochugen gift-giving seasons; status symbol
