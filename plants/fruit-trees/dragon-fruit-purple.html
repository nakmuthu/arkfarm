Dragon Fruit - Purple

Hylocereus costaricensis

Common Names: Purple Dragon Fruit, Red Dragon Fruit, Costa Rican Pitaya, Purple Pitaya

Local Name: Dragon Pazham (Tamil), Dragon Fruit (Hindi/English)

Family: Cactaceae

Origin: Costa Rica and Nicaragua; widely cultivated in tropical Asia

Plant Type: Perennial epiphytic cactus vine; climbing with aerial roots

Variety: Purple/red flesh variety; deep magenta-purple flesh; sweeter than white-fleshed types

Average Lifespan: 20–30+ years

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitClimbing epiphytic cactus; triangular, ribbed stems with small spines; climbs using aerial roots
Plant Size3–10 meters long; trained on posts or trellises at 1.5–2 meters height
LeavesNo true leaves; photosynthesis occurs in green triangular stems (cladodes); 3–5 cm wide
FlowersEnormous white flowers; 25–35 cm across; fragrant; open only at night; one of the largest cactus flowers
Flower_characteristics">Flower CharacteristicsNight-blooming; pollinated by moths and bats; each flower lasts only one night; hand pollination recommended
FruitOval to round; 10–15 cm; red-pink skin with green-tipped scales; deep purple-magenta flesh
Fruit ColorBright red-pink skin; deep purple to magenta flesh; numerous tiny black seeds throughout
Seed CharacteristicsNumerous tiny black seeds throughout the flesh; edible; 1–2 mm
Root SystemShallow fibrous roots plus aerial roots for climbing; drought-tolerant
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTropical and subtropical; warm, humid conditions preferred
Temperature Range20–38°C; frost-sensitive; optimal 25–35°C
Rainfall Requirement500–1500 mm; drought-tolerant; excess moisture causes root rot
Soil TypeWell-drained sandy loam; high organic matter; excellent drainage essential
Soil pH Preference6.0–7.0
Sun RequirementFull sun (6+ hours); tolerates partial shade
Spacing3–4 meters between posts; 2–3 plants per post
Irrigation MethodDrip irrigation; water deeply but infrequently; avoid waterlogging
Water RequirementLow to moderate; drought-tolerant; overwatering causes root rot
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, in large pots (40–60 liters) with post support; popular for terrace gardens
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleMonthly; balanced NPK; high potassium during fruiting; avoid excess nitrogen
Recommended Organic FertilizersVermicompost, neem cake, bone meal, wood ash, banana peel compost
Pesticide FrequencyAs needed; generally pest-resistant
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil for mealybugs and scale; copper fungicide for stem rot
Mulching PracticeOrganic mulch around base; helps retain moisture and suppress weeds
Training SystemTrain on concrete or wooden posts (1.5–2 m tall); allow stems to cascade over top
Pruning TimeAfter harvest; remove old, unproductive stems; thin crowded growth
How to PruneRemove stems older than 3 years; keep 4–6 productive stems per post; remove dead or diseased stems
Common PestsMealybugs, scale insects, ants (farming mealybugs)
Common DiseasesStem rot (Fusarium), anthracnose, bacterial soft rot in waterlogged conditions
Disease Prevention & RemediesExcellent drainage; avoid overwatering; copper fungicide for stem rot; remove infected stems
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsJune–November; multiple flushes; flowers open at night
Fruiting SeasonJuly–December; 5–6 fruiting cycles per year
Days to Harvest28–35 days from flowering to ripe fruit
Harvest IndicatorsSkin turns bright red-pink; scales turn slightly yellow; fruit gives slightly when pressed
Average Yield per Plant20–50 kg per post per year (mature)
Pollination TypeNight-pollinated by moths and bats; hand pollination recommended
Pollination NotesHand pollination at night dramatically improves fruit set; use brush to transfer pollen between flowers
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsNight-blooming; moth and bat pollinated; hand pollination at 8–11 PM improves yield significantly
Propagation by SeedsSeeds germinate in 2–3 weeks; slow to fruit (3–5 years); not recommended for variety maintenance
Propagation by CuttingsStem cuttings (30–40 cm) root easily; preferred method; fruits in 1–2 years; dry cut end before planting
Propagation by GraftingGrafting on Hylocereus undatus rootstock possible; used for difficult-to-root varieties
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability

Soil Conservation PracticesGrows on poor soils; minimal soil disturbance needed
Organic Practices UsedVermicompost-based feeding; minimal chemical inputs
Companion PlantsLeguminous cover crops; shade-tolerant plants between posts
Biodiversity BenefitsNight flowers attract moths and bats; supports nocturnal pollinator populations
