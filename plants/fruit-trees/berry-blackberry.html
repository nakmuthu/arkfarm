Blackberry

Rubus fruticosus

Common Names: Blackberry, Bramble, European Blackberry

Local Name: Jamun Ber (Hindi)

Family: Rosaceae

Origin: Europe, North America, temperate Asia

Plant Type: Perennial thorny shrub (bramble)

Variety: Erect, semi-erect, and trailing types; thornless cultivars available

Average Lifespan: 15–20 years (individual canes are biennial)

🌿 Botanical Description
Growth HabitVigorous arching or trailing bramble with biennial canes
Plant Size1–3 meters tall; trailing types up to 5 meters long
LeavesPalmately compound, 3–5 leaflets, serrated margins, dark green above
FlowersWhite to pale pink, 2–3 cm diameter, 5 petals in clusters
Flower CharacteristicsBorne on second-year canes (floricanes); mildly fragrant
FruitAggregate drupe, 1.5–3 cm long; composed of many small drupelets
Fruit ColorGreen to red to glossy black when fully ripe
Seed CharacteristicsSmall hard seed (pyrene) inside each drupelet
Root SystemPerennial root crown with spreading lateral roots; suckers freely
☀ Growing Conditions
ClimateTemperate to subtropical; needs winter chill for fruiting
Temperature Range15–25°C ideal; requires 200–700 chill hours below 7°C
Rainfall Requirement750–1500 mm annually; consistent moisture during fruiting
Soil TypeWell-drained, fertile loam rich in organic matter
Soil pH Preference5.5–6.5
Sun RequirementFull sun (6–8 hours daily)
Spacing1–2 meters between plants; 2–3 meters between rows
Irrigation MethodDrip irrigation preferred; avoid wetting foliage
Water RequirementModerate to high during fruiting; 25–50 mm per week
Can it Grow in Pots?Yes, compact varieties in 20–30 liter pots with trellis
🌾 Cultivation & Care
Fertilizer ScheduleEarly spring and after first harvest; avoid late-season feeding
Recommended Organic FertilizersCompost, aged manure, blood meal, kelp meal
Pesticide FrequencyMonitor regularly; treat as needed
Recommended Organic PesticidesNeem oil, pyrethrin, insecticidal soap, Bt for caterpillars
Mulching PracticeThick organic mulch (straw, wood chips) to retain moisture and suppress weeds
Training SystemT-trellis or V-trellis; separate primocanes from floricanes
Pruning TimeAfter harvest — remove spent floricanes; tip-prune primocanes in summer
How to PruneCut fruited canes to ground; thin new canes to 5–8 per plant; tip at 1.5 m
Common PestsSpotted wing drosophila, raspberry beetle, aphids, spider mites
Common DiseasesAnthracnose, cane blight, botrytis fruit rot, orange rust
Disease Prevention & RemediesGood air circulation, remove old canes, copper sprays, resistant varieties
🌸 Flowering & Fruiting
Flowering MonthsMay–June (temperate); varies in tropical highlands
Fruiting SeasonJune–September; primocane types can fruit into autumn
Days to Harvest40–60 days from flowering
Harvest IndicatorsFruit turns fully black, softens slightly, and detaches easily
Average Yield per Plant3–8 kg per plant per season
Pollination TypeSelf-pollination; also insect-pollinated
Pollination NotesBees greatly improve fruit size and uniformity; plant near bee habitat
🌱 Pollination & Propagation
Pollination DetailsSelf-fertile; bees and other insects improve drupelet set
Propagation by SeedsPossible but slow and variable; cold stratification needed
Propagation by CuttingsRoot cuttings (5–10 cm) in winter; tip layering in late summer
Propagation by GraftingNot commonly grafted; tip layering and root division preferred
💧 Environmental Impact & Sustainability
Water UsageModerate; efficient with drip irrigation and mulching
Soil Conservation PracticesDense root system prevents erosion; good for slope stabilization
Organic Practices UsedComposting, cover cropping, biological pest control
Companion PlantsTansy, garlic, comfrey, clover (nitrogen fixer)
Biodiversity BenefitsProvides food and shelter for birds and wildlife; flowers support pollinators
💰 Economic Importance
Commercial Production RegionsUSA (Oregon, California), Mexico, Serbia, UK, Chile
Market UsesFresh fruit, frozen berries, juice
Processing UsesJam, jelly, wine, pie filling, yogurt, smoothies
Market Value (Approx.)₹400–800 per kg (India); $3–6 per pint (USA)
🌿 Cultural Significance
SymbolismAssociated with protection in Celtic folklore; thorns symbolize resilience
Traditional UsesLeaves and roots used in folk medicine for sore throats; fruit in traditional preserves and wines
🍈 Nutritional Information
Calories43 kcal per 100g
Dietary Fiber5.3 g per 100g
Vitamin C21 mg per 100g
Vitamin A214 IU per 100g
Iron0.62 mg per 100g
Potassium162 mg per 100g
AntioxidantsVery high in anthocyanins, ellagic acid, and quercetin
Other Key NutrientsVitamin K, manganese, folate, vitamin E
🌿 Medicinal Uses
Anxiety & SleepNot a primary medicinal use
Immune SupportVitamin C and anthocyanins boost immune function
Anti-inflammatory PropertiesEllagic acid and anthocyanins show strong anti-inflammatory effects
Heart HealthFiber and antioxidants support cardiovascular health; may reduce LDL oxidation
Digestive HealthHigh fiber aids digestion; leaf tea traditionally used for diarrhea
